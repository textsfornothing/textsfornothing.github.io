We've had 30 years to deal with climate change. Instead, carbon emissions have doubled. Business as usual is no longer possible—at most, we get a few more decades before things start to collapse. We can choose a rapid, controlled transition to a zero-emission economy, or we can choose disaster. Those are the options.

Facts

More than half of all industrial-era carbon emissions have happened since 1988, when the IPCC was founded. 71% of all emissions since 1988 can be traced to just 100 state and commercial fossil fuel producers.

Emissions are still increasing. After a plateau in 2014-2016, global emissions increased in 2017 and again in 2018, reaching a record high.

The next decade is critical. According to the IPCC, we need to reduce emissions by at least 45% by 2030 to prevent catastrophe. This is still possible, but it requires radical action.

Solutions

We can stop climate change.

An immediate fossil fuel phaseout, starting now, would likely keep us under 1.5°C. This requires that “all fossil fuel infrastructure—power plants, factories, vehicles, ships and planes—from now on are replaced by zero-carbon alternatives at the end of their useful lives.”

Significant improvements to energy efficiency can also play a major role.

The solutions are ambitious, but not impossible. What happens next is up to us.

Canada

Canada is responsible for 1.6% of global emissions.

Canada's annual emissions have increased by 19% since 1990. The UK's emissions decreased by 41% over the same period. Canada's emissions are now 50% higher than the UK's, even though the UK has a larger economy and almost twice as many people.

Alberta and Saskatchewan have increased their emissions by 17% since 2005, mostly due to an increase in emissions from the tar sands. The rest of Canada decreased its emissions by 15% over the same period, with Ontario leading the way (mainly by eliminating coal). Alberta and Saskatchewan are now responsible for half of Canada's emissions.

Canada currently gives out about $3.3 billion in subsidies to oil and gas producers. This includes tax breaks as well as direct cash infusions. It does not include the federal government's $4.5 billion purchase of the Trans Mountain pipeline in 2018.

The transition away from fossil fuels will likely lead to a sharp decline in demand by 2035. This will have a devastating impact on Canada's fossil fuel industry and on the economy as a whole.
